# SECTION 15 — Interview Preparation (50 Questions + Model Answers)

**Method:** Use STAR (Situation–Task–Action–Result) with honest scope. Never invent patient outcomes or numbers.

---

### 1. Tell us about yourself.
I am Maliha Masud, a Doctor of Physiotherapy graduate from The University of Faisalabad with a CGPA of 3.58/4.00. Since December 2024 I have been a Physiotherapy House Officer at Madina Teaching Hospital, working under supervision across musculoskeletal, neurological and post-operative rehabilitation. I focus on thorough assessment, exercise-based rehabilitation, appropriate manual therapy and modality use, clear patient education and accurate documentation within a multidisciplinary team. I also supported clinical teaching in OPD. I am preparing international registration and English evidence for practice pathways in countries such as the UK, Germany, Australia, New Zealand and Canada.

### 2. Why physiotherapy?
I chose physiotherapy because it combines clinical science with practical problem-solving that restores people’s independence. I am motivated by measurable functional change—mobility, confidence, return to daily activity—achieved through assessment, exercise and education rather than passive care alone.

### 3. Why this organisation / country?
Adapt: I want to practise in a system that prioritises patient safety, evidence-based rehabilitation and multiprofessional teamwork. Your [service] aligns with my supervised experience in [MSK/neuro/post-op] and my plan to complete [HCPC/Ahpra/PBNZ/CAPR/Anerkennung] correctly before practising independently.

### 4. What are your clinical strengths?
Assessment structure, exercise prescription, patient education, documentation discipline, and calm multidisciplinary communication. My teaching-support experience also strengthened my ability to explain techniques clearly.

### 5. What is your biggest weakness?
As an early-career clinician, my independent caseload experience is still developing. I compensate by practising within competence, seeking supervision early, documenting carefully, and following a structured CPD and registration plan rather than overstating skills.

### 6. Describe your internship.
At Madina Teaching Hospital I assess and treat patients under supervision across MSK, neurological and post-operative pathways. I contribute to rehabilitation plans, deliver exercise and modality-supported care where indicated, educate patients, document progress, and collaborate with doctors and nurses.

### 7. How do you assess a new patient?
I begin with consent and a structured subjective history, screen for red flags, complete relevant objective testing, synthesise findings into clinical problems and goals, then agree a plan with the patient and document clearly for the team.

### 8. Give an example of multidisciplinary teamwork.
In the teaching hospital, physiotherapy goals need to align with medical plans and nursing care. I communicate assessment findings, escalate concerns, and adjust rehabilitation timing around procedures or medical status so care remains coordinated and safe.

### 9. How do you handle a patient in severe pain?
I validate the experience, reassess for red flags or post-operative complications, modify load and technique, use appropriate pain-relief adjuncts within protocol and competence, and escalate to the medical team if the presentation is unexpected.

### 10. How do you educate patients with low health literacy?
I use plain language, short instructions, demonstration–teach-back, written or pictorial HEPs where available, and caregiver involvement when appropriate, checking understanding before ending the session.

### 11. What is evidence-based practice to you?
Integrating best available evidence, clinical expertise, and patient values. For my stage, that means using assessment findings, applying interventions I am competent to deliver, and updating practice through CPD and literature rather than habit alone.

### 12. Tell us about a CPD activity that changed your practice.
My neurological assessment workshop on stroke and cerebral palsy sharpened how I structure neuro examination and differential thinking. I treat it as foundational learning that I continue to consolidate under supervision—not as specialist certification.

### 13. Have you done dry needling?
I completed a one-day university hands-on workshop on advanced dry needling techniques in September 2023. I do not present myself as an independently certified dry-needling practitioner.

### 14. How do you document care?
I record assessment findings, plan, interventions, patient response and education provided in line with hospital standards, so colleagues can continue care safely and progress can be reviewed.

### 15. Describe a challenging case (anonymised).
Choose a real pattern without identifiers: e.g., a post-stroke patient with shoulder discomfort and fear of movement. Task: support safe assessment and graded mobility. Action: coordinated with team, educated patient/caregiver, progressed exercise within tolerance. Result: improved engagement with therapy sessions under supervision. Reflect on what you learned.

### 16. How do you prioritise a busy caseload?
Safety first (unstable/red-flag patients), then time-sensitive post-operative needs, then planned sessions—communicating with the team when capacity is exceeded.

### 17. What does patient safety mean in physiotherapy?
Practising within competence, screening contraindications, safe handling, infection control, accurate records, escalation of deterioration, and honest communication about risk and uncertainty.

### 18. How do you respond to feedback from a supervisor?
I listen without defensiveness, clarify expectations, adjust practice immediately, and reflect so the learning transfers to the next patient.

### 19. Why should we hire an internationally trained graduate?
Because I bring a complete DPT academic foundation, teaching-hospital supervised practice, professional membership, teaching-support experience, and a transparent registration plan. I am realistic about supervision needs and serious about standards.

### 20. What is your registration status?
I am not yet registered with [HCPC/Ahpra/PBNZ/provincial college]. I am preparing documents, English evidence and the formal application pathway. I will not use a protected title unlawfully.

### 21. How is your English?
I currently function at B2 and am preparing for IELTS/OET at the score required by the regulator. In clinic I communicate assessment and education clearly and use teach-back to confirm understanding.

### 22. Are you willing to relocate / work shifts?
Yes, subject to visa and registration. I understand hospitals may require early, late or weekend patterns depending on service needs.

### 23. How do you manage cultural differences?
With respect, curiosity and non-assumption. I adapt explanations, use interpreters when needed, and keep the patient’s values central to goal setting.

### 24. Tell us about your research.
My research title concerns prevalence of myofascial trigger points in the unaffected shoulder of subacute hemiplegic patients and association with muscle tone. I can provide full citation details; I value linking clinical observation with enquiry.

### 25. What interests you in neurological rehabilitation?
Restoring function after neurological injury requires careful assessment, graded task practice, education and team coordination. My internship and neuro workshop exposure strengthened this interest.

### 26. What interests you in MSK?
MSK care rewards precise assessment and progressive loading. I enjoy helping patients understand their condition and regain movement through active rehabilitation.

### 27. How do you prescribe exercise?
Based on assessment findings, tissue healing stage, patient capacity and goals—starting with technique quality and tolerance, then progressing load, complexity and function.

### 28. When would you not use electrotherapy?
When contraindicated (e.g., relevant implants, malignancy concerns, impaired sensation depending on modality, acute infection, patient refusal) or when active rehab alone is more appropriate. I follow protocol and supervisor guidance.

### 29. How do you obtain consent?
Explain purpose, procedure, benefits, risks and alternatives in understandable language; check questions; proceed only with agreement; respect refusal.

### 30. Describe conflict with a colleague and resolution.
Use a real low-stakes example if available: differing opinions on session timing. Action: discuss privately, focus on patient need, agree plan, involve senior if needed. Result: clearer coordination.

### 31. How do you stay up to date?
CPD workshops, reading, reflective notes, supervision discussions, and professional membership updates through PPTA/World Physiotherapy.

### 32. What are NHS values / equivalent organisational values?
Compassion, respect and dignity, improving lives, teamwork, everyone counts—expressed clinically through listening, safe care, inclusion and collaborative practice. (Map to local values outside UK.)

### 33. How would you handle a complaint?
Listen, apologise for the experience of distress, avoid defensiveness, escalate per policy, document facts, support open learning, and prioritise patient safety.

### 34. What is clinical governance?
The framework ensuring care is safe, effective, accountable and improving—through standards, audit, training, incident learning and clear responsibility.

### 35. Have you led or taught others?
Yes—supporting clinical teaching in Physio OPD (Oct 2025–May 2026), demonstrating assessment approaches and reinforcing professional standards for students and junior interns.

### 36. How do you measure success in rehab?
Patient-centred functional goals, objective reassessment, adherence, and safe progression—not only pain scores in isolation.

### 37. What would you do if asked to work beyond competence?
Decline the specific task, explain the competence boundary, seek supervisor support, and help find a safe alternative so the patient is not abandoned.

### 38. How do you support discharge planning?
By communicating mobility status, HEP readiness, caregiver training needs and follow-up recommendations to the team early.

### 39. Why is documentation an ethical issue?
Poor notes break continuity, hide deterioration, and undermine accountability. Good notes protect patients and professionals.

### 40. What manual therapy do you use?
Entry-level techniques within training and supervision. I have workshop exposure to Kaltenborn, Maitland and Mulligan concepts and apply only what I am competent and permitted to perform.

### 41. How do you manage time with talkative patients?
Set agenda kindly at the start, use structured questions, summarise, and schedule education priorities so clinical essentials are completed.

### 42. What IT systems have you used?
Microsoft Office, Google Workspace, and telemedicine platform familiarity. I learn new EHR systems quickly with training.

### 43. Where do you see yourself in five years?
As an internationally registered physiotherapist with consolidated junior-to-mid competence, a clear MSK or neuro focus, audit/teaching contribution, and portable professional good standing.

### 44. Are you applying elsewhere?
You may say you are exploring aligned opportunities while remaining particularly interested in this role because of [specific service feature].

### 45. What salary do you expect?
For early-career roles I expect the published band/scale for the post and location; I am primarily focused on supervised development and correct registration.

### 46. How do you look after your own wellbeing?
Sustainable routines, peer support, supervision, and recognising early signs of overload so patient care quality remains protected.

### 47. What makes a great physiotherapist?
Clinical reasoning, honesty about limits, communication that changes patient behaviour, teamwork, and relentless attention to safety and evidence.

### 48. How would your supervisor describe you?
Reliable, teachable, documentation-focused, respectful with patients, and proactive in asking for clarification.

### 49. Do you have questions for us?
Yes—supervision structure; caseload mix; CPD support; registration assistance for internationally educated AHPs; success measures for the first six months; team MDT patterns.

### 50. Final pitch — why you?
Because I combine a strong DPT academic record, real teaching-hospital internship across core pathways, clinical teaching support experience, World Physiotherapy-linked membership, and a disciplined plan for language and registration. I will not overclaim. I will learn quickly, practise safely, and contribute to your patients with professionalism from day one.
